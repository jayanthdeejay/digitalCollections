Mouth of Platte River

1804 Jul 21
"The wind lulled at seven o'clock, and we reached, in the rain, the mouth of the great river Platte…Captains Lewis and Clarke ascended the river in a periogue, for about one mile, and found the current very rapid; rolling over sands, and divided into a number of channels; none of which are deeper than five or six feet."

Lewis, Meriwether, 1774-1809 and Clark, William, 1770-1838.
History of the Expedition Under the Command of Captains Lewis and Clark, to the Sources of the Missouri...
Philadelphia: Bradford and Inskeep, 1814. 2 Vols. Vol. 1, p. 30.

In 1804, the Mouth of the Platte River was a frontier, the line of demarcation between the Lower and Upper Missouri River as well as the beginning of Indian Territory. At Council Bluffs, on the west bank a few miles north of the Mouth of the Platte, Lewis and Clark held their first council with the Indians, representatives of the Otto and Missouri tribes.

River junctions are strategic locations and often the location of .trading establishments Clark declared the site an excellent one for fortification. Fort Atkinson was built on Council Bluffs in 1819 and a trading post was built at Bellevue, just above the Mouth of the Platte in 1809. In the 1830's the post at Bellevue was operated by the American Fur Company and also housed the Indian Agency to the Oto, Omaha, Pawnee and Iowa nations. Fort Atkinson, abandoned in 1827, has been reconstructed. Bellevue agency remains as a few tumbled foundation stones in Fontenelle Forest.

George Catlin (1796-1872) painted a view of the Mouth of the Platte in 1832 on his journey up the Missouri River on the maiden voyage of the American Fur Company steamboat Yellowstone from St. Louis to Fort Union at the Mouth of the Yellowstone River. During his six months on the river, Catlin painted hundreds of images in an effort to record people and lands which would soon vanish before the advance of civilization.

The following year Maximilian, Prinz von Wied (1782-1867) traveled up the Missouri River, also on the Yellowstone, going as far as Fort McKenzie at the Mouth of the Marias River. He was engaged in a similar, if more organized project to document the Native Americans of the Northern Plains. Accompanying him was Karl Bodmer (1809-1893), the Swiss artist hired to provide the visual record. Maximilian's Travels in the Ineterior of North America in 1832-1834 appeared in German, French, and English editions between 1839 and 1843 and were accompanied by an atlas of 81 aquatints derived from Bodmer's watercolors.
