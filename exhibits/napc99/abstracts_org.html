Cincinnati as Seen By Some of Her Early Engravers
  Virginius C. Hall, Curator of Rare Books Emeritus, Virginia Historical Society
Cincinnati's engravers tend to be overshadowed by her lithographers.   More than eighty engravers show up in Cincinnati city directories prior to 1860.   Their work, some signed, some not, appears in books and periodicals, on commercial paper and stationary.   It varies greatly in quality.   The firm of Doolittle & Munson turned out both elegant copperplate engravings and crude woodcuts.   Hotels, stores, and private schools were often depicted in the advertising pages of city directories.   These cuts, produced for commercial purposes together with promotional panoramas, street scenes and views of churches and other landmark buildings, provide a lively glimpse of downtown Cincinnati in the years between 1812 and the Civil War.

Emil Klauprecht, Ohio Valley German-American
  Richard F. Askren, Indianapolis, Indiana

Not long after the failed protest gathering in 1832 at Hambach Castles in Germany, teenager Emil Klauprecht left for the United States.   Soon after his arrival in Cincinnati, about 1836, Klauprecht and partner Adolph Menzel opened the Klauprecht and Menzel Lithography Company.   Peter Marzio credits them with producing Cincinnati's first lithograph in 1836.   Shaffer's Cincinnati City Directory for 1840 contains several examples.   Klauprecht's "Fliegende Blaetter" first appeared in 1846 and was highly decorated with excellent lithographs, many of these providing readers with graphic images of historical and current events that were frequently described in written form in the same number.   Klauprecht had an abiding personal interest in history.   The fine lithographs of Klauprecht and Menzel were seen not only in "Fliegende Blaetter" but also in their portrait series produced for the home.

In addition to his contributions to lithography, Emil Klauprecht was also noted for his accomplishments in the fields of music, literature, newspaper publishing and editing, history, and politics.

In 1864, President Abraham Lincoln named Emil Klauprecht U.S. Consul to Stuttgart in recognition for his efforts in behalf of the Republican Party.   Following his replacement, he remained in Europe until his death, in 1896.

John Casper Wild: Forgotten Artist of the American City
  John W. Reps, Professor Emeritus, Cornell University

J.C. Wild produced some of the finest and rarest American city views.   Born in Switzerland, Wild studied art in Paris where he lived for ten years and practiced as a landscape artist.   Coming to America in 1831, he worked first in Philadelphia, then in Cincinnati and again in Philadelphia before moving to St. Louis in 1839.   In 1844 he listed Davenport, Iowa as his place of residence, and he died there in 1846.

During this fifteen-year period Wild's paintings and prints captured the appearance of cities in an era before railroads and industrialization changed their appearance and altered their character.   His many prints of Philadelphia, six paintings of Cincinnati, numerous lithographs of St. Louis and nearby places on the Mississippi and his lost images of Galena, Illinois and Davenport, Dubuque and Muscatine, Iowa are unsurpassed in their detail and pictorial quality.

Despite articles by Martin Snyder, John Francis McDermott, and portions of chapters in Reps, Saint Louis Illustrated and Cities of the Mississippi, Wild remains virtually unknown.   This paper may bring an important figure of the American urban scene to the attention of a wider audience than his artistic achievements.

The Curious Case of the Hydra-Headed Stones: Ehrgott, Forbriger & Co.'s Civil War Portraits
  Christopher W. Lane, Co-Owner, Philadelphia Print Shop, Ltd.

In the midst of the Civil War, the Cincinnati lithographic publishing firm of Ehrgott, Forbriger & Co. produced a series of over 70 portraits of federal and state government officials and Union military and naval officers.   These prints are of particular interest because the facial portrait of each of the prints was based on a life photograph, but portraits of different individuals share the same bodies and the same backgrounds.   There is strong evidence which indicates that when these prints were first issued they were sold not just as separate prints, but also bound together in portfolios.

A study of the character of the prints, of similar portraits by other lithographic firms, and of the nature of the portrait portfolios offers insight into the history of mid-nineteenth century lithographic publishing.

Promotion Through Playing Cards
  Ronald Decker, Curator, Playing Card Museum, The United States Playing Card Company
After brief introduction to the history of standard playing cards, a few secondary uses will be considered.   The greatest attention will be given to customized decks for commercial distribution, those that promoted specific goods, services, people, places and events.   Nearly all of the given examples were printed by the United States Playing Card Company and its antecedents.   Thus, the decks have been arranged chronologically so as to mesh with the chronicle of the company, up until 1900.   Slides will illustrate the key artifacts.

The Smithsonian in Cincinnati: Exhibiting Prints at the Ohio Valley Centennial Exhibition
  Helena E. Wright, Curator, Graphic Arts Collection, National Museum of American History, Smithsonian Institution

In 1888 the Smithsonian exhibited nearly 1,000 prints at the Ohio Valley Centennial in Cincinnati.   Drawn from the newly formed Section of Graphic Arts of the U.S. National Museum and from generous loans by artists and publishers, the exhibition presented both traditional media and the new photomechanical processes, everything from Old Master engravings to modern halftones and photogravures.

This paper will discuss the print exhibition in the context of the 19th century trade fairs and international expositions that mingled fine art, commercial art, and popular culture and expanded the audience for the Cincinnati venue as part of the Smithsonian's program for public education.
